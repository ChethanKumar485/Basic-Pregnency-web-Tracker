🗣️ The Talk Test

A simple way to gauge exercise intensity during pregnancy:

Moderate Intensity ✅

You can carry on a conversation while exercising. Breathing is elevated but not laboured. Aim for this level!

Examples: brisk walk, casual swim, prenatal yoga, light cycling

Vigorous Intensity ⚠️

Difficult to speak in full sentences. Use with caution — only if you were active before pregnancy and your provider approves.

Examples: running, fast cycling, high-intensity interval

✅ Safe Exercise During Pregnancy
🚶‍♀️

Brisk Walking

Safest exercise at any trimester. 30 min/day meets weekly goals.

🏊‍♀️

Swimming

Excellent full-body, joint-friendly exercise. Reduces swelling.

🧘‍♀️

Prenatal Yoga

Improves flexibility, reduces back pain, calms anxiety. Avoid deep inversions.

🚴‍♀️

Stationary Cycling

No fall risk. Keeps heart rate in moderate zone. Safer than outdoor cycling.

💃

Low-impact Aerobics

Prenatal dance or aerobics classes designed for pregnancy.

🏋️‍♀️

Strength Training

Light weights, proper form. Avoid heavy loads and holding breath.

🤸‍♀️

Stretching

Reduces muscle tension, improves circulation. Do daily.

🧗‍♀️

Pilates (Modified)

Strengthens core safely. Avoid supine positions after week 16.

🚫 Activities to Avoid
  • Contact sports (basketball, soccer, rugby)
  • Activities with high fall risk (skiing, horseback riding, gymnastics)
  • Scuba diving (gas bubbles risk to baby)
  • Hot yoga / hot tub (overheating risk — keep body temp below 38.9°C)
  • High-altitude exercise (>8,000 ft / 2,500 m) without acclimatization
  • Lying flat on back after week 28 (compresses vena cava)
  • Exercises with Valsalva maneuver (heavy breath-holding lifts)
  • Exercising in extreme heat or humidity
  • Any activity causing pain, dizziness, or contractions
  • Exercising if you have placenta previa, preterm labor risk, or severe anemia
⚠️ Always stop exercising and call your provider if you experience: chest pain, shortness of breath before exertion, dizziness, headache, vaginal bleeding, fluid leaking, or decreased fetal movement.
💜 Pelvic Floor (Kegel) Exercises

Daily Kegel exercises strengthen the muscles supporting the uterus, bladder, and bowels — reducing incontinence risk and aiding postpartum recovery.

How to Do Kegels

  1. Empty your bladder first
  2. Tighten pelvic floor muscles (as if stopping urine flow)
  3. Hold for 3–5 seconds, then relax for 3–5 seconds
  4. Repeat 10–15 times per set
  5. Do 3 sets per day
  6. Don't tighten buttocks, abs, or thigh muscles
  7. Don't hold your breath

Benefits

  • Reduces urinary incontinence (leaking with sneezing/coughing)
  • Supports growing uterus weight
  • Reduces risk of prolapse
  • Helps perineum stretch during birth
  • Speeds postpartum recovery
💡 Set a daily phone reminder for Kegel exercises — consistency is key!
